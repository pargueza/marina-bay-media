Google Ad Must-Haves for First Timers

Creating your first Google Ads campaign? Read on for tips on getting started the right way.

Have goals and set the right metrics

With Google algorithms constantly changing, creating goals and metrics can be intimidating. Here's what to focus on first:

Lifetime Value (LTV)= (average order size) * (purchase frequency) * (lifespan — how long a customer stays a customer)

After calculating LTV, it's time to figure out your customer acquisition cost (CAC). CAC is the measurement of what you should be spending to get customers in the door. 

Having a low LTV to CAC ratio — in the ballpark of 3:1 is generally thought of as good. Obviously, you want to spend as little as possible on getting new customers. 

However, there may be reasons to aim for a higher CAC. For example, perhaps you're selling high-ticket items and want to focus on "quality over quantity." Or you may have a smaller budget and are willing to spend more for targeted leads.

Once you get a sense of the amount you'd like to invest, it's time to set up your KPIs, conversion tracking, and metrics. Google Analytics has great built-in functionality, but you may want to use other tools.

Look beyond your audience

Understanding your audience is important — but defining it is only the first step. Great ad copy draws the reader in by targeting their desires, pain points, and other motivating factors. 

Put yourself in the shoes of your target clients and customers. Better yet, build tools to ask them what they want, what they like, and don't like. 

If you'd like to use data to inform your decisions, use cohort analysis. Cohort analysis is essentially noting data on how users behave as a distinct group. A cohort could be periodic — customers who purchase a product on a certain season — or it can be behavioral — customers who clicked on a social media ad and clicked on your phone number.

Client feedback programs, polls, and forms are also good ways to gather key learnings on what audiences may prefer to see in an ad.

Let the writers write

Ad copywriting without clear expectations is a doomed endeavor. The seasoned ad copywriter often encounters roadblocks in the form of unnecessary bureaucracy. 

Do 15 people really need to approve a 30-character headline? Probably not. A comprehensive copywriting and style guide is a great way to get writers, editors, and other stakeholders on the same page. 

Leverage tech with a human touch

Generative AI and similar platforms may sound like a great tool for creating ad content, but there is a risk of (1) plagiarizing content unintentionally and (2) being unoriginal.

Your audience is human — so take the time to create ads that speak to their core needs. Today's most successful ads have several elements: 

  • Attention-grabbing headlines and descriptions
  • Relevant search terms and keywords
  • Unique value proposition and selling points

Don't neglect the landing page

A common frustration online users face is getting to a page that does not match the ad content. You only have a few seconds to capture their attention, so be sure to incorporate plenty of calls to action and the exact information they are looking for. 

Use tools such as Looker Studio to monitor the success of your campaigns — sharing key learnings with your team on at least a weekly basis to improve performance. A/B testing is a great way to maintain consistency while optimizing for the best-fit mix of assets, headlines, descriptions, and calls to action.

Remember compliance

Google Ads policies are constantly changing so be sure to double-check whether you may be advertising for a potentially controversial topic. Common problem areas include personalized health, copyrights and trademarks, gambling, alcohol, and adult content.

What now?

As you embark on your Google Ads journey, you may notice that there is a lot of change occurring at the moment. Primarily, there has been a major move to Google Analytics 4 (GA4). This is an updated method of tracking user engagement across your web properties that's quickly changing the state of SEM advertising. 

How will Google Analytics 4 affect Google Ads? The first is measurement. New metrics like engaged sessions, engagement rate, and engagement time will allow you to hone in on where users are in the customer journey — and develop tactics at scale to rein them in. GA4 also features a predictive audiences feature that will allow you to target likely purchasers or engage your target users at scale.
